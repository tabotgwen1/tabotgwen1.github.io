Our History

Ambazonia is the name used by separatists for the English-speaking regions (Northwest & Southwest) of Cameroon, stemming from its history as Southern Cameroons, a British territory after WWI, named after Ambas Bay where English was first settled in 1858. After a 1961 UN-supervised vote, Southern Cameroons joined French Cameroon, but growing Francophone dominance and loss of autonomy led to resentment, culminating in a 2017 unilateral declaration of independence and ongoing armed conflict with the Cameroonian government, known as the Anglophone Crisis.

The Journey

Colonial Roots (1858-1961)

British Settlement: The name "Ambazonia" comes from Ambas Bay (Ambozes), where English missionaries established a settlement in 1858, later Victoria (now Limbe).

German Control: Britain ceded the territory to Germany in 1887, making it part of German Kamerun.

British Mandate: After WWI, Germany lost its colonies; Britain administered Southern Cameroons as a UN Trust Territory, separate from Nigeria.

Unification & Growing Tensions (1961-2016)

1961 Referendum: Southern Cameroonians voted to join the French-speaking Republic of Cameroon, forming a federation.

Loss of Autonomy: The central government increasingly centralized power, replacing the federation with a unitary state in 1972, which Anglophones viewed as a betrayal.

Marginalization: Francophone dominance in politics and development fueled Anglophone grievances, with early calls for autonomy or independence emerging.

The Anglophone Crisis & Declaration (2016-Present)

Escalation: Protests in 2016-2017 over the marginalization of Anglophone lawyers and teachers were met with force.

Declaration of Independence: On October 1, 2017, separatists unilaterally declared the Republic of Ambazonia, leading to widespread clashes and armed conflict.

Ongoing Conflict: The Cameroonian government continues to control the territory, but armed separatist groups fight for independence, resulting in a protracted crisis.
